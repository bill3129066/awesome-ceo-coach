## Type 1 vs Type 2 Decision Classification

**Source**: Mochary Ch.11 (from Bezos 2015 Shareholder Letter)

**SOP**: Before spending time on any decision, classify it first.

| | Type 1 (One-way door) | Type 2 (Two-way door) |
|---|---|---|
| Reversible? | No — consequences are permanent | Yes — can undo or iterate |
| Who decides? | CEO, with full deliberation | Delegate to high-judgment individual |
| Speed | Slow, methodical | Fast — bias toward action |
| Process | RAPID with full input gathering | 5-minute discussion or unilateral |

**Common mistake**: Treating Type 2 decisions as Type 1. This causes slowness, risk aversion, and failure to experiment.

**Questions to classify**:
1. If this decision turns out wrong, can we reverse it within 2 weeks?
2. Is the cost of being wrong < the cost of delayed decision?
3. Will we learn something valuable even if it fails?

If yes to any → Type 2. Decide now.

## Above the Line / Below the Line Check

**Source**: Mochary Ch.16, Appendix A (from "15 Commitments of Conscious Leadership")

**SOP**: At the start of any coaching session or difficult conversation, check which state you're in.

**Above the line**: Open, curious, committed to learning
**Below the line**: Closed, defensive, committed to being right

**Diagnostic questions**:
1. "Right now, am I more interested in being right, or in learning?"
2. "Am I defending my position or genuinely curious about alternatives?"
3. "Am I blaming someone, or taking responsibility for my part?"

**If below the line**:
- Acknowledge it: "I notice I'm below the line right now."
- Pause. Breathe. Don't make decisions from this state.
- Ask: "What can I learn from this situation?"

**Coaching application**: If the CEO is ranting about a team member, below-the-line indicators include: blame language, certainty ("he always..."), and emotional charge. Name it: "I hear a lot of certainty. Are you above or below the line right now?"

## Energy Audit Quick Scan

**Source**: Mochary Ch.9

**SOP**: Monthly (or when the CEO reports feeling overwhelmed).

1. Print last week's calendar
2. Mark each hour: GREEN (gave energy) or RED (drained energy)
3. No neutrals — every hour must be one or the other
4. Look for patterns in RED hours
5. For each RED pattern, ask: "Can this be delegated, eliminated, or restructured?"

**Target**: 75%+ GREEN hours = Zone of Genius

**Zone classification**:
- Zone of Incompetence: Others do it better → Outsource
- Zone of Competence: You're fine but others are equally good → Outsource
- Zone of Excellence: You're great but don't love it → DANGER ZONE — delegate despite resistance
- Zone of Genius: Uniquely good AND you love it → Maximize time here

## Impeccable Agreement Audit

**Source**: Mochary Ch.13

**SOP**: When commitments are being dropped or trust is eroding.

An impeccable agreement has ALL of:
1. ✅ Specific deliverable (not vague "work on X")
2. ✅ Specific due date (not "soon" or "ASAP")
3. ✅ Explicit acceptance from the person committing ("Yes, I commit to delivering X by Y")
4. ✅ Consequences defined (what happens if broken — not punishment, but clarity)

**Audit process**:
- List all current commitments between CEO and team
- Check each against the 4 criteria
- Flag any that fail criteria → These will be broken. Fix them now.

**When an agreement is broken**:
1. Acknowledge it immediately
2. Clean it up: "I broke my agreement to deliver X by Y. I apologize."
3. Recommit with a new date, or renegotiate the agreement
4. Never let a broken agreement go unacknowledged — it erodes trust compoundingly

## No Single Point of Failure Check

**Source**: Mochary Ch.22

**SOP**: Quarterly, or when a key person is overloaded.

For every critical function in the company, answer:
1. Who is the primary owner?
2. If they were hit by a bus tomorrow, who could take over?
3. Is the backup person documented and informed?
4. Is there written documentation of the process?

**If any function has no backup** → This is a ticking time bomb.

**Mitigation options** (in order of preference):
1. Cross-train someone on the function
2. Document the process thoroughly (so anyone could follow it)
3. Reduce dependency on that function
4. Accept the risk explicitly (document that you know)

## Prioritization: Urgency vs Importance

**SOP**: When the CEO has "too many things to do."

Use Eisenhower Matrix with Mochary's Top Goal overlay:

| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | Do NOW (but minimize time here) | TOP GOAL — schedule 2hrs/day |
| **Not Important** | Delegate or batch | Eliminate |

**The trap**: Spending all time in Urgent+Important, zero time in Not-Urgent+Important. This is reactive mode. The CEO must PROTECT Top Goal time.

## Stakeholder Mapping

**SOP**: Before any important meeting, pitch, or negotiation.

| Stakeholder | What they want | What they fear | Your leverage | Relationship temp |
|-------------|---------------|----------------|--------------|-------------------|
| {name} | | | | Hot / Warm / Cold |

**For each stakeholder, prepare**:
1. Your opening (Bailey: build trust first, never pitch cold)
2. Your ask (specific, bounded)
3. Your BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement — see Bailey BATNA article)

## Root Cause vs Symptom Diagnosis

**SOP**: When the CEO presents a problem, before prescribing a solution.

Ask 5 times: "Why?"
1. Problem: "My co-founder didn't deliver the integration by Friday"
2. Why? → "They're overcommitted on 4 workstreams"
3. Why? → "We never formally prioritized their tasks"
4. Why? → "We don't have AORs or a priority system"
5. Why? → "We're a small team and assumed we didn't need one"

**Root cause**: Lack of formal prioritization system (not your co-founder being slow).
**Framework to apply**: AORs (Mochary Ch.21) + Priority system
**Symptom fix** (also do, but don't stop here): Help your co-founder reprioritize this week's tasks
