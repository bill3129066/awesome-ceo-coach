## Meeting Types & Cadence

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 24

| Meeting | Cadence | Duration | Purpose |
|---------|---------|----------|---------|
| **1-on-1** | Weekly | 25-50 min | Direct report's agenda, their issues, not yours |
| **Team Meeting** | Weekly | 50-90 min | Written updates, Issues & Proposed Solutions, decisions |
| **All-Hands** | Monthly/Quarterly | 50 min | CEO presents company state, takes live Q&A |
| **Office Hours** | Weekly | 50 min | Open door, anyone can bring anything |
| **Offsite** | Quarterly | 1-2 days | Strategic planning, team bonding, retrospective |

## 1-on-1 Meetings

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 24

The 1-on-1 belongs to the DIRECT REPORT, not the manager. They set the agenda. The manager's job is to listen, coach, and unblock.

**SOP: 1-on-1 Structure** (50 min):
1. **Check-in** (5 min): "How are you feeling on a scale of 1-10? Why that number?"
2. **Their agenda** (30 min): Issues they want to discuss. They should write these before the meeting.
3. **Your agenda** (10 min): Things you need from them (keep this short)
4. **Action items** (5 min): Summarize commitments from both sides

**Manager's role during 1-on-1**:
- Listen more than talk (target: 70% them, 30% you)
- Ask "What do you need from me?" more than "Here's what I need from you"
- Address concerns immediately. Don't say "I'll think about it."
- Use Impeccable Agreements for any commitment made

For Bailey's perspective on reducing 1-on-1 frequency as trust builds, see [Dave Bailey: Why Founders Are Reducing Their 1-1s](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/why-founders-are-reducing-their-1-1s).

## Team Meetings

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 24

**SOP: Team Meeting Structure** (90 min):
1. **Read updates** (10 min): Everyone wrote updates before the meeting. First 10 min = silent reading.
2. **Issues & Proposed Solutions** (60 min): Written format. 5 min per issue max. Consensus or escalate to RAPID.
3. **Action items** (10 min): Recap all decisions + DRIs + due dates
4. **Feedback on meeting** (10 min): Like/Wish That format, continuously improve the meeting itself.

## Feedback: Giving

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 25

**SOP for giving feedback**:
1. Ask permission: "I have some feedback. Is now a good time?"
2. State the behavior (observable, specific): "In the meeting, you interrupted Sarah three times."
3. State the impact: "She stopped contributing after that."
4. Make a request: "Next time, could you let her finish before responding?"

**Mochary's specific phrases**:
- "I noticed that..." (not "You always...")
- "The impact was..." (not "You made people feel...")
- "My request is..." (not "You should...")

For Bailey's deeper guide on feedback, see [Dave Bailey: Give Feedback](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/give-feedback).

## Feedback: Receiving

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 25

**SOP for receiving feedback**:
1. Say "Thank you" (nothing else initially)
2. Do NOT defend, explain, or justify
3. Ask clarifying questions: "Can you give me a specific example?"
4. Reflect back: "What I heard is... Is that right?"
5. Take time to process if needed: "I want to think about this. Can we revisit tomorrow?"

**The hardest part**: Not defending. Your brain screams "but the context was..." Resist. Just listen. Process later.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Make 1-on-1s the direct report's meeting, not yours
- Write updates BEFORE team meetings. No verbal monologues.
- Give feedback with the behavior, impact, request formula
- Receive feedback with "Thank you" first, process later
- End every meeting with explicit action items (DRI + due date)
- Run Like/Wish That on the meeting process itself

❌ **Don't**:
- Let anyone present an issue they haven't written up
- Spend >5 min on one issue without resolution. Escalate to RAPID.
- Give feedback in public that should be private
- Cancel 1-on-1s. This signals "you're not important."
- Let meetings run without an agenda. That's just a conversation.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: CEO needs to give feedback to VP Engineering about consistently missing sprint commitments.

**Step 1: Prep**: Use the Facts, Impact, Request framework from team-dynamics.md.

**Step 2: Ask permission**: "Chris, I have some feedback about our recent sprint cycles. Is now a good time?"

**Step 3: Behavior**: "In the last three sprints, the team committed to ten story points each time, but we only delivered five. This includes the database migration and the API refactor that we discussed as top priorities."

**Step 4: Impact**: "This means our product roadmap is slipping, and I can't give reliable updates to the board about when we'll ship the new features. It's creating uncertainty for the sales team as well."

**Step 5: Request**: "My request is: during sprint planning, I need you to be more conservative with what the team commits to. I'd rather see us commit to five points and hit them all than commit to ten and miss half. If a delay looks likely mid-sprint, please flag it to me immediately."

**Step 6: Listen**: Let Chris respond. Don't re-argue. If he has context about unexpected technical debt or resource issues, say "Thank you, I didn't have that context. Let's look at how we can account for that in the next planning session."
