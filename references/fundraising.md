## The Relationship Method

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 27

Fundraising is not about pitching. It's about building genuine relationships with investors before you need money.

**SOP: 4 Steps**:
1. **Build a target list** of 50+ investors who invest in your stage and sector.
2. **Get warm intros** via Triangulation (see below).
3. **Build the relationship** over 3 to 6 months. Share updates, ask for advice, and show progress.
4. **Ask for the investment** only when you have traction and the relationship is warm.

**Key insight**: The best fundraises happen when investors ask to invest, not when founders ask for money. Build relationships early enough that investors come to you.

## Build Trust & Like

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 27, Ch. 29

Trust is the foundation of every investor relationship. Build it through genuine curiosity, not impressive slides.

**SOP: First 5 minutes of any investor meeting**:
1. Get curious about them. "What are you most excited about in your portfolio?"
2. Find genuine connection. Look for shared interests, mutual contacts, or similar experiences.
3. Ask about their challenges. "What's the hardest part of investing in this space?"
4. Do not pitch in the first 5 minutes.

**The underlying principle**: People invest in people they trust. Trust scales with proximity. Face-to-face is better than video. Video is better than phone. Phone is better than email.

For Bailey's approach, see [Dave Bailey: Build Investor Relationships](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/build-investor-relationships) and [Dave Bailey: How To Engineer The Perfect Investor Meeting](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/how-to-engineer-the-perfect-investor-meeting).

## Triangulation for Intros

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 27

Never cold-email an investor. Always get a warm intro from someone the investor respects.

**SOP**:
1. Identify target investor.
2. Find who in your network knows them. Use LinkedIn, ask your other investors, or ask advisors.
3. Ask the connector if they'd be comfortable making an intro. Offer to draft the email for them.
4. Write the double opt-in intro email. Make it effortless for them.

**The draft intro template**:
"[Connector] here. I'd like to introduce you to [Founder], CEO of [Company]. They're building [one sentence]. [Specific reason this investor should care]. I'll let [Founder] follow up directly."

## SAFEs vs Priced Equity

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 27

**SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)**: Investor gives money now and gets equity at the next priced round. Faster and cheaper (around $2K legal). No board seat. Use for pre-seed and seed.

**Priced Equity**: Formal valuation, share price, and board seats. More expensive ($20K to $50K legal) with more investor protections. Use for Series A and beyond.

**SOP for choosing**: If raising less than $2M and pre-Series A, use a SAFE. If raising more than $2M or have significant traction, use a priced round.

## Timing: Inflection Points

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 27

Raise money at inflection points. These are moments when your story is most compelling.

**Examples of Inflection Points**:
- **Product Launch**: Transitioning from "we might build this" to "people are using this."
- **Marquee Win**: Signing a pilot with a industry leader or a major platform partnership.
- **Growth Milestone**: Hitting a specific user count or revenue run rate that proves the model.
- **External Validation**: Getting into a top-tier accelerator or winning a major industry award.

**How to recognize and prepare**:
1. **Identify your next 3 milestones**: What are the specific achievements that will change how an outsider sees the company?
2. **Track the data**: Ensure you have clean metrics showing the "before" and "after" of the inflection point.
3. **Warm the leads 2 months early**: Start the relationship building before the milestone hits. Tell them: "We're about to hit [Milestone] in 6 weeks. I'll send you an update then, but wanted to get to know you first."
4. **Strike while the iron is hot**: Launch the formal raise immediately after the milestone is confirmed.

**The worst time to raise**: When you're running out of money and have no recent wins. Start 6 months before you need the money.

## Legal Cost Management

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 27

Lawyers are expensive. They often bill for as many hours as possible.

**SOP: The 4-Hour Meeting**:
Before engaging a lawyer for a complex deal, schedule a 4-hour in-person or intensive video meeting. Review every single term and document in that block. Pay for those 4 hours only. This replaces weeks of back-and-forth emails and can save 5 to 10 times the total legal fees.

**Legal Do's and Don'ts**:
- **Do** use standard documents like the YC SAFE whenever possible.
- **Do** use solo practitioners or boutique firms for everything except lead investor docs in a major priced round.
- **Do** ask for a fixed-fee quote for standard work like incorporation or SAFE issuance.
- **Don't** use a "Big Law" firm for seed-stage work. They'll over-engineer the documents and charge 10x the market rate.
- **Don't** CC your lawyer on every email. Every "Reply All" they read costs you money.
- **Don't** let lawyers negotiate business terms. Their job is to document the deal you've already agreed on.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Start building investor relationships 6 months before you need money.
- Always get warm intros. Never cold-email.
- Lead with curiosity about the investor, not your pitch.
- Use SAFEs for early-stage to save time and money.
- Send monthly investor updates even before raising. It builds trust.
- Draft the intro email for your connector. Make it easy.

❌ **Don't**:
- Pitch in the first 5 minutes of any meeting.
- Raise when desperate. Investors can smell it.
- Use big law firms for routine legal work.
- Accept investment from someone you don't trust or respect.
- Hide bad news from investors. Transparency builds trust faster than good news.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: CEO wants to restart a paused conversation with a Tier 2 VC after shipping a key product milestone.

**Step 1: Assess relationship temperature**: Review the last interaction. Why did it pause? Was there a specific metric they wanted to see? Ensure the "door was left open."

**Step 2: Triangulation check**: See if your original connector can provide a quick "nudge." If not, prepare a direct but low-pressure outreach.

**Step 3: Build the ask**: Focus on the update, not the money. "When we last spoke, we were still working on [Feature/Metric]. We've since shipped that and seen a [Percentage] increase in engagement. I'd love to get your thoughts on how this changes our market position. Do you have 15 minutes next week?"

**Step 4: Prep the meeting**: Lead with curiosity. Ask about their recent investments or their view on the current market. Then show the new data. Ask for feedback on your roadmap, not a check.

**Step 5: Follow-up**: Send a summary within 2 hours. Include the specific data point they found most interesting.
