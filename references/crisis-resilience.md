## Runway Management

**Source**: [Dave Bailey: Startup Runway](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/startup-runway)

**SOP — Monthly runway check**:
1. Cash on hand ÷ monthly burn rate = months remaining.
2. If runway is less than 6 months: enter crisis mode. Every decision must filter through "does this extend runway or accelerate revenue?"
3. If runway is less than 3 months: emergency mode. Cut all non-essential spending and focus solely on the survival path.

**Two levers**: You can reduce burn (cut costs) or increase income (revenue or fundraising). Most founders only think about fundraising, but cutting costs is faster, more reliable, and entirely within your control.

## Cost Cutting Framework

**SOP — When runway is less than 6 months**:
1. List ALL expenses in order of size.
2. For each expense, ask: "If we cut this today, what's the worst that happens in 90 days?"
3. If the answer is "nothing critical" → cut it immediately.
4. If the answer is "we lose a key capability" → keep it, but find a way to reduce the cost by 50%.

**Priority cuts**:
- Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) tools that are "nice to have."
- Events and conferences (unless they're directly and provably generating a sales pipeline).
- Contractors doing non-critical or secondary work.
- Office space (consider going remote or moving to a coworking space).

**Last resort cuts**:
- Salary reductions (the CEO's salary must be the first to go).
- Headcount reduction (layoffs are painful, so do them once and do them deep).

## Pivot Evaluation SOP

**Source**: Composite from Bailey's pivot/persevere methodology

Pivoting is a fundamental shift in strategy, not just a small change in tactics.

**When to consider a pivot**:
- You have spent 3 to 6 months iterating, but the retention numbers aren't improving.
- The cost to acquire a customer (CAC) is consistently higher than their lifetime value (LTV).
- Customers use your product but for a completely different purpose than you intended.

**Step-by-Step Pivot Process**:
1. **Identify the Core Failure**: Is the product bad? Is the market too small? Is the pricing wrong? Be specific.
2. **Gather Evidence**: Don't pivot on a hunch. Use direct customer feedback and quantitative data.
3. **List the Options**: What is the smallest change you can make? Can you sell the same product to a different market? Can you sell a different product to the same market?
4. **Define the Success Criteria**: What needs to be true in 30 days for this new direction to be "working"?
5. **Commit Fully**: Once you decide to pivot, kill the old product and strategy. Don't try to run both at once.

**Good Reasons to Pivot**:
- You've discovered a much larger problem while trying to solve the first one.
- The market has shifted fundamentally (e.g., new regulation or technology).
- Your product works but has a low ceiling for growth.

**Bad Reasons to Pivot**:
- The founders are bored with the current problem.
- You're panicking because of a temporary setback.
- You're chasing a "hot" new trend without a clear advantage.

## Mental Resilience Under Pressure

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 10, Appendix A

A crisis erodes judgment. Your mental state is the company's operating system. If your "OS" is crashing, the company will, too.

**SOP during a crisis**:
1. **Prioritize Physical Health**: Exercise, sleep, and good food are non-negotiable. They are the fuels for your decision-making engine.
2. **Name the Emotion**: Simply saying "I'm scared we're going to run out of money" out loud is better than carrying unnamed anxiety.
3. **Controllable vs Uncontrollable**: Make a list of every problem. Cross out everything you cannot affect (e.g., the global economy). Spend 100% of your energy on the items that remain.
4. **The Power of the Peer Group**: Talk to other CEOs who have been through similar crises. Isolation is a choice, not a requirement.
5. **Make Decisions in Daylight**: Never make an important decision when you're tired, hungry, or emotional. If it's 11 PM and you're panicking, go to sleep and decide at 8 AM.
6. **Conscious Leadership**: Check if you're "above the line" (curious, open, learning) or "below the line" (defensive, closed, right). You cannot solve a crisis from below the line.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Check your runway monthly and adjust your burn rate proactively.
- Cut costs before they become urgent. The CEO's salary should be the first cut.
- Name your emotions during a crisis. Unnamed fear leads to poor judgment.
- Maintain your health routines, especially when things are going wrong.
- Communicate transparently with your team about the company's financial state.

❌ **Don't**:
- Pivot out of panic without specific evidence.
- Hide the truth from your team. They will find out, and you'll lose their trust.
- Make big decisions when you're exhausted or highly emotional.
- Cut the people or tools that are directly generating revenue to "save money."
- Sacrifice your sleep for "the hustle." It's a false trade that leads to mistakes.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: The CEO of a B2B startup has 4 months of runway. The main product launch is delayed by 6 weeks due to a technical bug, and the lead investor for the current round has gone quiet.

**Step 1 — Triage**: "Let's be precise. You have 4 months of cash. Your monthly burn is $20K. If the launch is delayed, you won't have revenue until month 3 at the earliest. This is an emergency."

**Step 2 — Burn Reduction**: "Cut the CEO salary to $0 immediately. Cancel the upcoming marketing off-site. Pause the contract for the PR agency. This adds 3 weeks of runway."

**Step 3 — Strategic Pivot**: "You're waiting for a bug fix. In the meantime, what can you sell? Can you offer a consulting version of your tool or a 'concierge' service for your top 5 leads? Use these 6 weeks to generate cash, not just wait."

**Step 4 — Investor Management**: "Stop emailing the lead investor. They sense the panic. Instead, focus 100% on closing one of those concierge deals. Send an update only when you have new traction to report. That's how you win back their interest."
