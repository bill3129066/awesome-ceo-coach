## Product-Market Fit Definition

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 2, Ch. 30

Product-Market Fit (PMF) means customers find so much value in your product that they buy it and recommend it to others.

**Metrics that indicate PMF**:
- **Revenue**: Recurring revenue from multiple, non-affiliated customers.
- **Renewal rates**: Customers continue to pay after their initial contract or trial.
- **Net Promoter Score (NPS)**: A score above 50 is a strong signal.
- **Retention**: A flat retention curve over time.

**Warning**: Enterprise customers often have "innovation budgets" to test new tech. An initial pilot contract doesn't mean you have PMF. Only renewals and expansions prove real value.

**The biggest mistake**: Thinking you have PMF before you actually do, then scaling the team and spending. This kills more startups than moving too slowly.

**SOP**: Keep the team small (≤6 people) until you have clear evidence of PMF.
- **3 people**: CEO + 2 Engineers.
- **6 people**: CEO + 3 Engineers + Designer + Ops.

For Bailey's perspective, see [Dave Bailey: Product Market Fit](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/product-market-fit).

## Customer Obsession SOP

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 17

Deeply understand real customers and their problems. Not what you *think* they need, but what they actually need.

**The SOP**:
- **Talk to customers weekly**: Not monthly, and never just through surveys. Aim for at least 3 to 5 direct conversations every week.
- **Ask the right questions**:
    - "What is the #1 problem you're trying to solve in your workflow today?"
    - "How are you solving it now? How much time or money does that cost you?"
    - "What is the one thing this product *must* do for you to consider it a success?"
    - "If we didn't exist, what would you use instead?"
- **Synthesize feedback**: Don't just act on the loudest voice. Look for patterns across multiple conversations. Record your calls and use a tool to transcribe them for easy review.
- **Avoid the "Feature Trap"**: Customers are great at telling you their problems but often terrible at designing the solution. Listen to the pain, not just the feature request.

## Target Beachhead: The Normandy Strategy

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 30

A "beachhead" is a small, specific market segment where your product is 10x better than the legacy solution. Like the invasion of Normandy, the goal isn't to take the whole country on day one. It's to win a small, defensible piece of land first.

**How to Choose Your Beachhead (SOP)**:
1. **Segment the Market**: Break your potential market into smaller groups (e.g., "Small design agencies using Figma" instead of just "Designers").
2. **Evaluate Segments**: Use these criteria to rank them:
    - **Pain intensity**: How much does the problem hurt this specific group?
    - **Accessibility**: Is it easy to reach these people (e.g., a specific Slack group or conference)?
    - **Size**: Is the segment large enough to sustain you for the next 12 months?
    - **Competition**: Are they ignored by larger incumbents?
3. **Pick One**: Focus all engineering and marketing resources on that single segment. Say no to everyone else until you've dominated it.

## Product Manager Role

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, "Product" section

The Product Manager (PM) sits between customers and engineering. Their job is to ensure the team builds the right things in the right order.

**Responsibilities**:
1. **Understand customer problems**: Translate pain points into technical requirements.
2. **Assess technical feasibility**: Work with engineering to know what can be built and how long it will take.
3. **Prioritize**: Use a value-versus-effort matrix. Focus on high-value, low-effort features first.
4. **Create Specs**: Provide clear wireframes and functional documents so engineers don't have to guess.

**Reporting Structure**: Product must report to the CEO, never to Engineering or Sales. If Product reports to Engineering, the customer's voice is often lost in favor of technical elegance. If it reports to Sales, the roadmap becomes a series of one-off features for the next deal.

## Scaling (When to Grow)

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 2

Scale after you have PMF, not before. When you have repeatable revenue and a clear target segment, you're ready to grow.

**The Scaling Process**:
1. **Secure Funding**: Raise enough capital to support the next 18 to 24 months of growth.
2. **Hire in Order**: Marketing → Sales → Customer Success → DevOps → Engineering. This ensures you can support the new customers you're bringing in.
3. **Implement the Formal Management System**: At 15 to 20 people, you can no longer manage by "walking around." You need a system that includes:
    - **Defined AORs**: Everyone knows exactly what they own.
    - **Weekly 1-on-1s**: Regular feedback loops between managers and reports.
    - **Company OKRs**: Clear goals that everyone is working toward.
    - **KPI Dashboard**: A shared view of the metrics that matter.
4. **Standardize Onboarding**: Create a wiki and a set of docs so new hires can become productive in their first week.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Talk to customers directly and weekly.
- Stay small (≤6 people) until you have clear PMF evidence.
- Focus all resources on a single beachhead segment.
- Prioritize features based on customer value, not engineering interest.
- Ensure the Product role reports directly to the CEO.

❌ **Don't**:
- Confuse a pilot project or "innovation budget" with PMF.
- Scale the team before you have repeatable revenue.
- Build features no one specifically asked for.
- Spread your resources across multiple market segments at once.
- Let engineering decide the roadmap without direct customer input.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: The CEO of a developer tools startup is debating whether to add complex enterprise features (like SSO and audit logs) or fix bugs in the free tier that's seeing high churn.

**Step 1 — PMF Check**: "Do you have PMF with the free tier? No, because the churn is high. If people are leaving, you haven't solved their core problem yet."

**Step 2 — Priority Assessment**: "Fix the free tier first. Enterprise features are useless if the core product doesn't work well enough to retain users. You're trying to scale a leaky bucket."

**Step 3 — Beachhead Check**: "Who is your current target? If it's individual developers at mid-sized startups, they don't care about SSO yet. They care about the product not crashing when they run a build. Focus on the pain that's causing the churn."
