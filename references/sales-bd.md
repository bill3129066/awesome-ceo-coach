## Trust-first Sales

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 29

The same "Build Trust & Like" method from fundraising applies to sales. Before you sell, build genuine trust through curiosity.

**SOP: The Meeting Flow Structure**:
A successful sales or partnership meeting should follow a 3-part structure.

**Part 1: Discovery (The first 40%)**
Spend this time understanding their world. Do not pitch. Ask open-ended questions to find the pain.
- "What are your top 3 priorities for the quarter?"
- "Where is your team spending the most manual effort right now?"
- "What's the biggest risk you're managing?"

**Part 2: The Bridge (The middle 20%)**
Summarize what you've heard. "It sounds like you're trying to [Goal] but [Challenge] is causing [Pain]. Is that right?" This shows you've listened and ensures alignment.

**Part 3: The Solution (The last 40%)**
Present only the parts of your product or partnership that solve the pain identified in Part 1. Transition with: "Based on what you said about [Challenge], here's how we can help."

**How to transition from Discovery to Pitch**:
Don't just start talking. Ask for permission first. "I think I have a good sense of what you're dealing with. Would it be helpful to see how we've solved this for others?" This shifts the dynamic from you pushing to them pulling.

## Customer Development Script

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 29

Before selling, understand the customer deeply. Use these questions:

1. "What are your goals for [their domain] this year?"
2. "What challenges are making those goals hard to achieve?"
3. "If you had a magic wand, what would the ideal solution look like?"
4. "What solutions have you tried? What worked? What didn't?"
5. "What would solving this be worth to you?"

**Why this order matters**: Goals to Challenges to Ideal solution moves them from what they want to what they need. Only then do you position your product.

## Sell Results, Not Features

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 29

| ❌ Features | ✅ Results |
|------------|-----------|
| "Our platform has a built-in CRM integration." | "You'll save 10 hours a week by eliminating manual data entry." |
| "We use a proprietary machine learning algorithm." | "Your team will see a 20% increase in lead conversion accuracy." |
| "24/7 priority support and dedicated account manager." | "Your system will stay online and any issues will be fixed in under 15 minutes." |

**SOP**: For every feature you want to mention, ask "So what?" until you reach the result the customer actually cares about.

## Lead Generation (Seeds/Nets/Spears)

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 29, from Aaron Ross "Predictable Revenue"

| Type | Method | Pros | Cons |
|------|--------|------|------|
| **Seeds** | Word-of-mouth, referrals | Close fast, best customers | Hard to scale proactively |
| **Nets** | Marketing, events, SEO, content | Scalable, cost-effective | Slow to build, broad targeting |
| **Spears** | Direct outbound (email, LinkedIn) | Predictable, hyper-targeted | Needs dedicated reps, may not be profitable at small deal sizes |

**SOP for early-stage**: Start with Seeds (network referrals), then add Spears (targeted outreach). Nets come later when you have content and resources.

## Target Beachhead Strategy

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 30, the Normandy analogy

Don't attack the entire market. Find the one segment where your solution is 10x better than legacy, and concentrate all resources there.

**SOP**:
1. Segment the market into customer types.
2. For each: how underserved are they? How good is our fit?
3. Pick the one segment where our advantage is greatest.
4. Dominate that segment before expanding.

**The Normandy principle**: The Allies didn't spread across every beach. They concentrated at one weak point and expanded from there.

## Negotiation Fundamentals

**Source**: [Dave Bailey: Negotiation](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/negotiation), [Dave Bailey: BATNA](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/BATNA)

**BATNA** (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement): Know your walk-away option before entering any negotiation. The stronger your BATNA, the more power you have.

**SOP: Negotiation Prep**:
1. **Identify Leverage Points**: What do they want most? What is their BATNA?
2. **Prepare Concessions**: List things you're willing to give up (like a lower price) and what you want in return (like a longer contract or a case study). Never give a concession without getting one.
3. **Set the "Anchor"**: If you have good data, make the first offer. It sets the range for the rest of the conversation.

**Specific Phrases to Use**:
- **To slow things down**: "That's an interesting proposal. I'll need to discuss this with my team and get back to you."
- **To trade**: "I can't do [Price], but if you can commit to a 2-year term, I can get closer to that number."
- **To test the walk-away**: "It seems we're quite far apart on [Term]. Is that a deal-breaker for you?"
- **The "Flinch"**: When they state a price, don't say anything. Just look slightly surprised and wait. Often they'll start negotiating against themselves.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Lead every sales conversation with curiosity about their problems.
- Sell results, not features. Always answer "so what?"
- Know your BATNA before any negotiation.
- Start with Seeds (referrals), then add Spears (targeted outbound).
- Focus on one beachhead segment. Don't spread thin.

❌ **Don't**:
- Pitch your product before understanding their pain.
- List features. Nobody cares about your tech stack.
- Enter a negotiation without knowing your walk-away point.
- Try to serve every market segment at once. Concentrate force.
- Let "I'm not a salesperson" be an excuse. The CEO is the first and best salesperson.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: CEO preparing for an integration partnership with a major platform.

**Step 1: Understand their goals**: What does the platform want? More user retention? A more complete ecosystem? Higher API usage? Research their recent public statements.

**Step 2: Customer Dev**: Prepare these questions for the partnership manager:
- "What's the biggest gap in your current integration ecosystem?"
- "What would the ideal user experience look like for someone using both our products?"
- "What metrics does your team use to measure the success of a new integration?"

**Step 3: Results framing**: Don't say "We have a REST API." Say: "Our integration will allow your users to complete [Task] 50% faster without leaving your platform, which will drive higher daily active usage for you."

**Step 4: The Proposal**:
1. Initial beta for selected power users.
2. Full launch with co-marketing.
3. Long-term roadmap for deeper feature sharing.

Lead with the beta. It's low-risk and proves value quickly.
