## Co-founder Fundamentals

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 02

- Solo founders have high burnout rates. Find a partner with complementary skills.
- Never create a 50/50 partnership. There is no way to break deadlocks. One person must be able to decide.
- Keep team ≤6 people until Product-Market Fit (>$1M ARR).
- Startups usually fail because they grow too EARLY, not too late.

**Bailey's psychological lens**: Co-founder relationships follow attachment patterns. Read [Dave Bailey: Co-founder Relationships](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/co-founder-relationships) for how attachment theory explains co-founder conflict and repair.

## Impeccable Agreements

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 13

An agreement is impeccable when ALL four criteria are met:

| Criteria | ✅ Good | ❌ Bad |
|----------|---------|--------|
| Specific deliverable | "Deliver the investor deck with updated financials as a PDF" | "Work on the deck" |
| Specific due date | "By Friday 6pm" | "Soon" / "ASAP" |
| Explicit acceptance | "Yes, I commit to this" | Silence / "I'll try" |
| Defined consequences | "If missed, we reschedule the partner demo" | Nothing stated |

**SOP for creating impeccable agreements**:
1. State the deliverable in concrete terms
2. State the deadline
3. Ask: "Can you commit to this? If not, what would you need?"
4. If the other person says "I'll try" then that is a NO. Renegotiate.

**SOP for broken agreements**:
1. Acknowledge immediately: "I broke my agreement to deliver X by Y."
2. Apologize without excuses
3. Recommit with a new date, or renegotiate
4. Never let it go unacknowledged. Unaddressed broken agreements erode trust compoundingly.

## Conflict Resolution (4 Steps)

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 15

When conflict arises between two people:

**Step 1: Anger**. Let the angry person express it in a safe setting. Don't fix, just listen. Anger needs to be felt before logic can work.

**Step 2: Underlying issue**. Ask "What is the real issue underneath the anger?" Usually it's fear: fear of failure, loss of control, not being respected, etc.

**Step 3: Request**. Help the person convert their complaint into a specific request: "What I need is ___."

**Step 4: Agreement**. The other person either agrees to the request, proposes a modified version, or explains why they can't. Either way, create an Impeccable Agreement.

**Bailey's tools for difficult conversations**: See [Dave Bailey: Tough Conversations](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/tough-conversations) and [Dave Bailey: Nonviolent Communication](https://dave-bailey.com/blog/nonviolent-communication).

**Conversation script for tough conversations** (Facts, Impact, Request):
1. **Facts**: State observable behavior, not interpretation. "You committed to delivering by Friday and it's now Monday."
2. **Impact**: State the effect. "This means we missed the partner demo deadline."
3. **Request**: Be specific. "Can you commit to Wednesday? If not, what do you need?"

## Conscious Leadership (Above/Below the Line)

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 16, Appendix A

At any moment, a leader is either **above the line** or **below the line**.

| Above the Line | Below the Line |
|---------------|----------------|
| Open, curious, committed to learning | Closed, defensive, committed to being right |
| "What can we learn from this?" | "Who's to blame?" |
| Takes radical responsibility | Points fingers |
| Speaks candidly | Withholds or gossips |

**The most important thing**: Knowing you're below the line is more important than being above it. Self-awareness first.

**SOP to shift above the line**:
1. Pause. Take a breath.
2. Ask: "Am I more interested in being right, or in learning?"
3. If you're below the line, name it: "I notice I'm below the line right now."
4. Ask: "What's the opposite of the story I'm telling myself? What if that's also true?"

**Core principles from the 15 Commitments**:
- Take radical responsibility (not blame)
- Learn through curiosity
- Feel all feelings through to completion
- Speak candidly. Reveal, don't withhold.
- Eliminate gossip. Talk directly to the person.
- Keep impeccable agreements
- Generate appreciation
- Live in play and rest
- Explore the opposite of your story

## Transparency & Eliminating Gossip

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 14

Share all relevant information with your team. The cost of information asymmetry always exceeds the cost of transparency.

**On gossip**: A statement about another person made with negative intent, or one you wouldn't say if they were in the room. If you gossip, clean it up by telling everyone involved. If someone gossips TO you, redirect: "Have you told them directly?"

## Culture

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 18

Culture is not what you say. It's what you tolerate. Every behavior you accept becomes the standard.

Build culture intentionally:
1. Define values with specific behaviors (not just words)
2. Hire for culture fit alongside skill
3. Address culture violations immediately
4. Lead by example. You ARE the culture at this stage.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Check all four criteria before calling something an "agreement"
- Address broken agreements within 24 hours. Don't let them fester.
- Name when you're below the line. Self-awareness is the first step.
- Use Facts, Impact, Request for every difficult conversation.
- Practice the "Explore the Opposite" on your strongest beliefs.
- Redirect gossip: "Have you told them?"

❌ **Don't**:
- Accept "I'll try" as a commitment. It's a polite no.
- Let anger bypass the 4-step conflict resolution. Anger is data, not a decision.
- Use transparency as a weapon. Share information to inform, not to punish.
- Ignore broken agreements because "it's awkward." Compounding trust erosion is more awkward.
- Wait until resentment builds to have difficult conversations. The best time is now.
- Create 50/50 decision-making structures. Someone must be able to break ties.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: A CEO feels frustrated because their co-founder keeps missing sprint commitments. They want to address it but fear damaging the relationship.

**Step 1: Above/Below the line check**.
"I hear the frustration in your voice. Check in with yourself: are you above or below the line right now?"
The CEO sighs. "Definitely below. I'm feeling defensive and annoyed."
"Good. You named it. That is the first step toward a shift. What is the story you're telling yourself about why they haven't delivered?"
"He doesn't care about our launch timeline as much as I do. I feel like he's being lazy."

**Step 2: Explore the opposite**.
"What if the opposite is true? What if he cares so much that he's paralyzed by technical debt? Could that be true?"
A pause follows. "Maybe. He does stay late every night."

**Step 3: Impeccable Agreement audit**.
"Let's look at the commitment. Did you create an impeccable agreement? Was the deliverable specific? Was there a due date your co-founder explicitly accepted?"
"I asked if we could finish the API by Friday, and he said it should be fine."
"That isn't impeccable. The deliverable was vague, there was no specific time, and 'should be fine' is a polite way of saying no. You didn't have a real agreement."

**Step 4: The conversation (Facts, Impact, Request)**.
"Here is how you handle the conversation:
- **Facts**: I asked for the API by Friday. It is now Monday and the work isn't finished.
- **Impact**: We missed our partner integration window because of this delay.
- **Request**: Can you commit to having the auth endpoints finished by Wednesday at 5pm? If that isn't realistic, let's look at the roadmap together right now."

**Step 5: Structural fix**.
"This will happen again unless you fix the system. Use Impeccable Agreements for every commitment moving forward. We should also do an AOR mapping to see if your co-founder is overloaded."
