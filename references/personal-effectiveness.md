## Getting Things Done (GTD)

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 03 — from David Allen

Every item that crosses your desk goes to one of these lists:

| List | What Goes Here | Review Cadence |
|------|---------------|----------------|
| **Next Actions** | Tasks written as single actions, split by context (Computer / Calls / Outside / Home) | Daily |
| **Waiting For** | Things delegated to others — person, action, date requested | Daily |
| **Someday/Maybe** | Things you want to do but not now | Weekly |
| **Agenda** | Topics to discuss with people you meet regularly — batched, not one-off messages | Weekly |
| **Projects** | Multi-step initiatives with serial next actions | Weekly |
| **Goals** | 10-Year Vision + Quarterly OKRs | Weekly |

**The 2-minute rule**: If an action takes <2 minutes, do it immediately. Don't put it on a list.

**SOP**: Process every item in every inbox (email, Slack, text, to-do) daily. For each: do it (<2 min), schedule it (Next Actions), delegate it (Waiting For), defer it (Someday/Maybe), or delete it.

## Inbox Zero

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 04

Your inbox is a triage room, not a waiting room. If you use it as storage, urgent items bleed out unseen.

**SOP**:
1. Check email TWICE daily (morning + afternoon). Not more.
2. Each time: process EVERY item to zero.
3. <2 min → do immediately
4. >2 min → write a Next Action, move email to correct GTD list
5. Repeat until inbox is empty

**Setup**: Use Gmail's Multiple Inbox feature (see Andreas Klinger's method) to create views for Next Action, Waiting For, Someday/Maybe, Reference.

## Top Goal

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 05 — from Greg McKeown's "Essentialism"

**The rule**: Schedule 2 hours each day to work on your Top Goal only. Do this every single workday. Period.

**SOP**:
1. Identify your #1 goal for the quarter
2. Block 2 hours EARLY in the day (before others compete for your attention)
3. During this time: NO email, NO Slack, NO calls
4. If 2 hours feels impossible, start with 30 minutes and increase weekly

**Why early**: Research shows we have more decision-processing energy early in the day when the brain is rested. Do the important work first.

**The trap**: Spending all day on urgent things and zero time on important things. The urgent never stops. You must PROTECT this time.

## On Time and Present

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 06

Every minute you're late steals productivity from everyone waiting. There's no winning scenario when you waste someone's time.

**SOP**:
1. If you'll be late, notify attendees BEFORE the meeting starts
2. Plan to arrive 15 min early for external meetings
3. Wrap up 5-10 min before your next meeting
4. Schedule 25-minute and 50-minute meetings (not 30/60) — Google Calendar has a setting for this
5. In meetings: phone face-down. No checking messages. Be present.

## Write It Down Rule

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 07

**The rule**: When you say something for the second time, write it down. You WILL say it again. Writing it saves everyone's time and improves quality.

Put it in the company wiki. If it's truly foundational, put it on a wall.

Encourage others to do this too: every time a question is answered on Slack, prompt the questioner to document the response in the wiki.

## Gratitude & Appreciation

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 08

**Gratitude** (inward): Say "I am grateful for ___" five times each morning. Be specific — names, actions.

**Appreciation** (outward): When you catch yourself feeling grateful for someone, tell them. Be specific: "Alex, I appreciate you for documenting our onboarding process and adding it to the wiki."

**When receiving appreciation**: The only correct response is "Thank you." Don't deflect.

**SOP**: Reserve 1 hour per week for follow-ups and outreach that include appreciations (from Chris Fralic, First Round Capital).

**Why this matters for performance**: We perform best when feeling good about ourselves. Specific positive feedback improves team performance measurably — even in competitive settings.

## Energy Audit & Zone of Genius

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 09 — from Gay Hendricks' "The Big Leap"

**Energy Audit SOP** (do monthly):
1. Print last week's calendar
2. Mark each hour GREEN (energizing) or RED (draining) — no neutrals
3. Look for RED patterns
4. For each pattern: can it be delegated, eliminated, or restructured?
5. Target: 75%+ GREEN = Zone of Genius

**Four Zones**:
| Zone | Definition | Action |
|------|-----------|--------|
| **Incompetence** | Others do it better | Outsource |
| **Competence** | You're fine but not special | Outsource |
| **Excellence** | You're great but don't love it | DANGER — delegate despite pressure from others who benefit |
| **Genius** | Uniquely good AND you love it, time disappears | Maximize time here |

**The trap**: Zone of Excellence. You're so good at it that everyone wants you to keep doing it. But it drains you. This is what you must have the courage to delegate.

## Health

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 10 — by Alex MacCaw (Clearbit)

All your work is for nothing if it costs your health.

**SOP**:
- Exercise multiple days per week — find what works (lifting, running, boxing)
- Get a trainer if self-motivation slips (company should pay — investors won't mind)
- Build a CEO support group of peers — learn to be vulnerable
- Get a therapist — even if you think you don't need one
- Meditate daily — use Calm/Headspace, set calendar reminders
- Set aside a room for meditation, make it visible to lead by example

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Process every inbox item daily — the 2-minute rule saves hours
- Protect 2 hours of Top Goal time every morning — no exceptions
- Do monthly Energy Audits until 75%+ is Zone of Genius
- Write things down the second time you say them
- Practice specific gratitude daily and specific appreciation weekly
- Schedule 25/50-minute meetings to create buffer time

❌ **Don't**:
- Check email more than twice a day — you're wasting hours
- Let urgent tasks eat your Top Goal time — the urgent never stops
- Stay in your Zone of Excellence because it feels safe — delegate it
- Give vague appreciation ("great job") — always be specific (who, what, why)
- Skip exercise because you're "too busy" — this is short-term thinking
- Treat your inbox as a to-do list — it's triage, not storage

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: A B2B SaaS CEO with 8 months of runway reports spending 70% of time on customer support tickets instead of closing enterprise deals. They feel overwhelmed and stuck in a loop of reactive work.

**Step 1 — Diagnose with Energy Audit**: "Let's look at your last week. Walk me through Monday hour by hour. Mark each block GREEN or RED based on whether it energized you or drained you."

Result: 80% RED. Most time spent on answering technical support tickets, manually correcting database entries for users, and triaging Slack messages from the product team.

**Step 2 — Identify Top Goal**: "What's your #1 goal this quarter?"
CEO: "Close three enterprise contracts to extend our runway."
Coach: "Good. How many hours this week did you spend directly advancing those specific deals?"
CEO: "...maybe 2 hours total? I was busy keeping users happy."
Coach: "That's your problem. Not the volume of tasks — the fact that your survival goal got 4% of your time."

**Step 3 — Prescribe**: "Starting tomorrow:
1. Block 8-10am as Top Goal time. Calendar event. No meetings. No support.
2. During that time, work ONLY on those enterprise deals — follow up with stakeholders, refine the proposals, or map out the implementation plan.
3. Check email and Slack at 10am and 3pm only. Process to zero each time using GTD.
4. Hire a part-time support lead or empower your lead engineer to handle the technical tickets. Your presence in support is now a risk to the company's survival."

**Step 4 — Zone of Genius check**: "Manually correcting database entries — is that your Zone of Genius?"
CEO: "No, I'm just fast at it, so it feels easier to do it myself than to fix the root cause or train someone."
Coach: "That's the Excellence trap. You're competent, but it's killing your capacity for the work only you can do. Write a Next Action to document the database fix process for the engineering team and stop doing it yourself."
