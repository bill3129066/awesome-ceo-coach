## Scorecard Method

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 28, Appendix C, from Geoff Smart's "Who"

Before recruiting, create a Scorecard:
1. **Mission**: One sentence describing the business problem this role solves.
2. **Outcomes**: 3 to 8 measurable results with due dates, ranked by importance.
3. **Competencies**: Traits required for success and culture fit.

**SOP**: Scorecard first, sourcing second. Never start recruiting without a clear scorecard.

## Interview Process

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Appendix C

4 stages, each with a specific purpose:

**1. Phone Screen** (15 to 30 min): Weed out quickly. Ask:
- "What are your career goals?"
- "What are you really good at professionally?" (push for 8 to 12 with examples)
- "What are you not good at?" (push for 5 to 8 real weaknesses)
- "How will your last 3 bosses rate you 0 to 10? Why?"

If not a clear A, pass. Better to miss an A player than waste hours on a B or C.

**2. Topgrading** (1 to 3 hours): Chronological walk-through of every job. For each: What were you hired to do? Biggest accomplishments? Low points? People you worked with (names, you'll reference-check them)? Why did you leave?

**3. Focused Interviews**: 3 interviewers, each covering a third of the scorecard outcomes and competencies.

**4. Reference Interviews**: The most accurate predictor. Do not skip. Use the candidate's bosses, peers, and reports. Do not use their supplied reference list.

## Selling Candidates

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 28

Once you find an A player, sell with **Fit, Family, Freedom, Fun**:
- **Fit**: Show how their goals align with the company's direction.
- **Family**: "What can we do to make this easy for your family?"
- **Freedom**: "We set OKRs collaboratively. I won't micromanage."
- **Fun**: Show what makes working here enjoyable.

## Firing Well

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Ch. 28

Firing is a failure of feedback or hiring. It should never be a surprise.

**SOP: The Process**:
1. **Direct Feedback**: As soon as performance drops, tell them. "I'm not seeing the results we discussed. Here's specifically what's missing."
2. **Expectations and Timeline**: Set clear, written goals for the next 30 to 60 days. Agree on how success will be measured.
3. **Weekly Check-ins**: Review the goals every week. If there's no improvement, remind them that their job is at risk.
4. **The Final Conversation**: When it's clear it's not working, keep it short and professional.

**The Conversation Script**:
- **The Hook**: "Today is your last day at [Company]."
- **The Reason**: "As we discussed, the performance goals for [Specific Task/Outcome] have not been met. We've decided to move in a different direction."
- **Next Steps**: "We'll be providing [Amount] of severance. Here's who you'll talk to about benefits and COBRA."
- **Listen and Dignity**: Let them speak, but don't debate the decision. Offer to help them find a better-fitting role elsewhere if their integrity was not the issue.

## Chief of Staff & Biz Ops

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, "Executive" section, "Biz Ops" section

**When to hire a Chief of Staff (COS)**:
Hire when you can't complete your weekly goals or too many low-level tasks drain your energy. A COS acts as your "force multiplier."

**How to hire a COS**:
Look for someone with 4 to 8 years of experience in management consulting or high-growth operations. They must be highly organized, emotionally intelligent, and a strong writer.

**How to onboard**:
1. **Full Access**: Give them access to your email, calendar, and all company documents.
2. **Shadowing**: Have them join every meeting for the first 2 weeks. They take notes and observe how you make decisions.
3. **Gradual Delegation**: Start with low-stakes tasks (scheduling, follow-ups). Move to project management, then to representing you in meetings.

**Biz Ops**:
Mini-CEOs who parachute into problem areas. They can take over project or people management while keeping the domain expert as the architect. This is useful when a manager isn't scaling or a department is in chaos.

## The Technical Founder Challenge

**Source**: Mochary, *The Great CEO Within*, Appendix B, by Alex MacCaw (Clearbit)

Technical founders must embrace sales, invest in customer success, and gradually delegate engineering:
- **0 to 15 people**: Stay heads-down coding.
- **15 to 20 people**: Start delegating. Find a CTO or VP of Engineering you trust absolutely.
- **20 to 30 people**: Code at most 1 day per week. Fill the remaining time with company building.

## Dos and Don'ts

✅ **Do**:
- Create a scorecard before starting any recruiting.
- Push for real weaknesses in interviews.
- Reference-check with the candidate's actual bosses.
- Give direct feedback before firing.
- Sell A players with Fit/Family/Freedom/Fun.

❌ **Don't**:
- Hire without a clear scorecard. You'll get who's available, not who you need.
- Skip reference checks. They're the most accurate predictor.
- Fire someone who was never given clear feedback and a chance to improve.
- Overlook integrity and culture fit for salespeople.
- Wait too long to fire. The team knows before you do.

## One-Shot Example

**Situation**: CEO considering first engineering hire to reduce bus factor on their sole technical co-founder.

**Step 1: Scorecard first**: Before posting anything, write the scorecard. Mission: "Ensure the technical stability and scalability of our core product while allowing the CTO to focus on high-level architecture and R&D." Outcomes: (1) Maintain 99.9% uptime for the production environment. (2) Take full ownership of the CI/CD pipeline and deployment process within 4 weeks. (3) Complete documentation for all critical systems within 8 weeks.

**Step 2: Reality Check**: Review your current runway. If you have less than 6 months of cash, can you afford the onboarding time? A new hire often slows things down for the first month. Is it better to find a part-time contractor or an advisor instead?

**Step 3: Source through Network**: Ask your advisors and other technical friends for referrals. Don't post a job on a generic board yet. Referrals have a much higher success rate for first hires.

**Step 4: Reference Check the "Co-founder Fit"**: When you find a candidate, do a deep reference check on how they work with founders. Do they challenge ideas respectfully? Do they take ownership without being told what to do?
